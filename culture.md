# Culture from the Soil: Reclaiming the True Roots of a People's Way of Life

When most people hear the word “culture,” they picture opera houses, leather-bound classics, oil paintings in gilded frames, and erudite discussions in hushed museum halls. This narrow understanding is not wrong—it is simply incomplete and, in many ways, historically recent. True culture, as the word itself reveals, is far more earthy, democratic, and intimate. It is the traditions of a people: how they grow and eat their food, how they dress, the rhythms of their language, the festivals that mark their seasons, and the unspoken rules that govern daily life. The elite version of culture—often state-funded, museum-curated, and agenda-driven—is merely one layer superimposed upon this deeper, organic reality. To understand why this distinction matters, we must return to the word’s roots and trace how its meaning has been stretched, narrowed, and contested over centuries.

The etymology of “culture” is revealingly agricultural. The English word entered the language in the mid-15th century meaning “the tilling of land, act of preparing the earth for crops.” It comes directly from Latin *cultura*, “a cultivating, agriculture,” which itself derives from the verb *colere*—“to tend, guard, till, cultivate, worship, or inhabit.” The same root gives us “colony,” “cult,” and “cultivate.” Cicero, in his *Tusculanae Disputationes* (45 BCE), first extended the metaphor philosophically with the phrase *cultura animi*—“cultivation of the soul”—using the image of farming the mind as one farms a field. By the 16th and 17th centuries in English, the term had begun to refer to the “cultivation of the mind” or personal refinement. Only later, in the 19th century, did it acquire the two senses we still wrestle with today: on one hand, the intellectual and artistic refinement of the individual or elite class; on the other, the collective customs and achievements of an entire people.

This dual evolution set the stage for the modern split between “high” culture and everything else. In 1869, Matthew Arnold famously defined culture as “the best which has been thought and said in the world.” For Arnold and the Victorian elite that followed him, culture was a civilizing force—an antidote to the anarchy of industrial masses. It was something to be acquired through education and exposure to the classics, opera, Shakespeare, and the fine arts. This conception still dominates popular imagination: a “cultured” person reads Proust, listens to Beethoven, and visits the Louvre. Museums, universities, and national theaters became the temples of this view, often subsidized by governments precisely because they could shape public taste and national identity. Yet this “official” culture embodies precisely the dynamic so sharply observed here: it is frequently paid for, curated, and imposed by the governing class to serve particular narratives—whether nationalist, ideological, or commercial.

In stark contrast stands the anthropological understanding that emerged at roughly the same time. In 1871, the pioneering anthropologist Edward Burnett Tylor offered what remains the most influential broad definition: “Culture or Civilization, taken in its wide ethnographic sense, is that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society.” Tylor deliberately used “culture” and “civilization” almost interchangeably and insisted the term encompassed the entire lived reality of a people—not merely its refined artistic peaks. This view was later radicalized by thinkers such as Raymond Williams, who insisted “culture is ordinary”: it is in the everyday language we speak, the meals we share, the clothes we wear to work or festival, and yes, the way we cultivate our crops and tend our land. Culture, in this sense, literally grows from the ground up.

This anthropological perspective aligns perfectly with the deeper insight under discussion. Folk culture—the traditions of ordinary people—predates and underlies elite culture. It is local, communal, and transmitted orally or by example across generations: the recipes passed from grandmother to granddaughter, the dialect that carries centuries of regional history, the harvest rituals tied to the agricultural calendar, the clothing adapted to climate and labor. Folk culture is not “popular culture” in the modern mass-media sense (which is often commercially manufactured and globalized); it is pre-industrial, rooted, and authentic to a specific place and people. Sociologists distinguish it sharply from both high culture (elite, canonized, often written and preserved in institutions) and mass/popular culture (commercially produced for broad consumption). Folk culture’s audience is small and local; its creators and consumers are the same community. It does not need state patronage or academic validation to exist—it simply *is* the life of the people.

The disparagement of this deeper culture as “popular,” “low,” or “folk” (often said with a sneer) is itself a relatively recent power move. In the 19th century, as industrialization and urbanization accelerated, elites sought to draw bright lines between “refined” and “vulgar.” High culture became a marker of class distinction, a form of cultural capital that could be wielded to maintain social hierarchies. Museums and academies preserved and elevated certain works while marginalizing others. Governments funded “national” arts that promoted unity or ideology—whether Soviet socialist realism, Nazi exaltation of “Aryan” folk motifs turned into propaganda, or today’s state-supported multicultural exhibits that sometimes prioritize political messaging over organic tradition. Critics from Antonio Gramsci to Pierre Bourdieu have described this as cultural hegemony: the ruling class does not merely dominate economically; it defines what counts as “legitimate” culture, thereby naturalizing its own values as universal. The “official” culture of museums and concert halls is thus often less a spontaneous flowering of human genius than a carefully tended garden designed to impress, educate, or pacify.

Yet the living culture of a people stubbornly resists total imposition. Language evolves on the street before it appears in dictionaries. Food traditions survive globalization (think of how Italian grandmothers still make Sunday sauce the way their ancestors did). Clothing, music, and festivals adapt but retain their roots. Even when elites attempt to “preserve” folk culture—turning it into sanitized tourist performances or museum exhibits—they cannot fully capture its vitality, which exists only in daily practice. The same root that gives us “culture” also gives us “agriculture”: both are acts of care, of tending something living over generations. A people’s culture is not a static exhibit behind glass; it is the ongoing cultivation of their shared life.

In an age of mass media and global homogenization, reclaiming the fuller meaning of culture is urgent. When we reduce the word to intellectuals, books, theatre, and classical music, we implicitly accept a hierarchy that devalues the wisdom embedded in everyday traditions. We forget that the farmer shaping the land, the grandmother teaching the old recipes, and the community speaking its dialect are all engaged in the original act of *cultura*. The elite arts have their place—they can elevate and inspire—but they are not the whole story, nor even the most authentic one. Culture grows from the soil of ordinary life. The governing class may fund the museums and write the grant proposals, but the true culture of a people is what they live, not what they are told to admire. To honor that truth is to return the word to its roots—literal and metaphorical—and to recognize that every people, in tending their fields, their families, and their customs, is already cultivating something profound.
