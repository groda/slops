# Things I Learned Today: From AI Slop to Military AI Engines (March 22, 2026)

- **People calling AI a "slop machine"**: This is a pretty common (and often derogatory) term that's blown up online, especially in the last couple of years. "AI slop" (or just "slop") refers to low-quality, low-effort, mass-produced content generated by AI tools—things like generic images, formulaic text, clickbait videos, or filler posts that flood social media, search results, YouTube, and other platforms. It's seen as digital clutter prioritizing speed, volume, and ad revenue over originality, accuracy, depth, or real value. The term draws from the idea of "slop" as cheap animal feed (leftover scraps), and it's often used to criticize the flood of mediocre generative AI output that's easy to spot (e.g., uncanny images, repetitive phrasing, or banal "realistic" styles). It even got named Word of the Year by Merriam-Webster and others in 2025 due to how pervasive the complaint became. Not all AI output is slop—people distinguish between thoughtful uses and the spam-like stuff—but yeah, it's a go-to insult for the junk side of the tech.

- **The "4D Framework for AI Fluency"**: This is a structured model developed by two professors: Rick Dakan (Ringling College of Art and Design, where he's AI Coordinator and a professor of creative writing) and Joseph Feller (University College Cork, professor of information systems and digital transformation). They created it in collaboration with folks like Anthropic to help people (especially in education, business, and nonprofits) build practical, responsible skills for working with AI. The four Ds stand for core competencies in human-AI collaboration:

  - **Delegation** — Knowing what tasks to hand off to AI (and when it's appropriate).
  - **Description** — Writing clear, effective prompts and instructions so AI understands what you need.
  - **Discernment** — Critically evaluating AI outputs for accuracy, bias, usefulness, etc.
  - **Diligence** — Taking ethical responsibility, maintaining oversight, and iterating responsibly.

  It's all about effective, efficient, ethical, and safe AI use rather than just "using tools." Anthropic has integrated it into some of their education/reporting work (like their AI Fluency Index), and it's gained traction in workshops, courses, and discussions around AI literacy.

- **US military using Palantir, and what AI engine/technologies it uses**: Yes, Palantir has deep ties to the US Department of Defense and various branches (Army, Air Force, Navy, etc.), and its role has ramped up significantly. As of March 2026, the Pentagon is formalizing Palantir's Maven system as a "program of record" (a core, long-term funded component of military AI strategy), making it a cornerstone for battlefield AI across services. Key systems and tech include:

  - **Maven Smart System** (often just called Maven): This is the flagship AI platform Palantir has run for Project Maven since around 2017. It's a command-and-control tool that ingests massive data from satellites, drones, sensors, radars, intelligence reports, etc., then uses AI/ML to analyze it in real time—automatically identifying targets (e.g., enemy vehicles, buildings, weapons), patterns, and threats. It supports targeting, decision-making, and Combined Joint All-Domain Command and Control (CJADC2). Recent expansions integrate it more widely, including for time-sensitive strikes and battle space awareness.

  - **Palantir's Artificial Intelligence Platform (AIP)**: This is the broader engine powering many of their defense offerings. AIP connects generative AI/large language models (LLMs) and other AI to operational data in secure, private networks (including classified ones and tactical edge devices like drones/ships). It enables building AI agents, automations, workflows, and apps with strong governance, security, and human oversight. Notably, Palantir integrated Anthropic's Claude models into AIP for government/intel/defense use (announced in late 2024), allowing chatbots to analyze intel, suggest plans, uncover insights, etc.—though with guardrails and human-in-the-loop requirements (no fully autonomous targeting). There have been some tensions around Anthropic/Claude being flagged as a potential supply chain risk by the Pentagon, but it's still in play.

  - Other notable tech: Systems like TITAN (Tactical Intelligence Targeting Access Node—AI-enabled mobile ground stations/trucks for the Army that fuse multi-domain data for precise targeting), Edge AI for austere environments (low-bandwidth ops), sensor fusion/orchestration engines, and integrations with partners (e.g., Anduril, L3Harris, Northrop Grumman).

  Palantir's approach emphasizes ontology-driven data unification, AI/ML for insights, and secure/ethical deployment in high-stakes military contexts.
