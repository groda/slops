# The Imposition Inherent in Solidarity: An Etymological Critique

**The origin of "solidarity" is baked into why the word so often feels like a demand rather than an invitation.**

The English word enters the language around 1829, straight from French *solidarité*. That was a deliberate coinage in the 1765 *Encyclopédie* (the big Enlightenment project), built on the older French legal adjective *solidaire*. And that, in turn, comes from Latin *in solidum* — a Roman-law term for **joint and several liability**. If a group of people owed money "in solidum," each one was on the hook for the **entire** sum, not just their share. You couldn't say "that's not my problem" — the debt was solid, whole, indivisible.

Latin *solidus*/*solidum* itself means "solid," "whole," "entire" (the same root as "solid," "consolidate," even the old Roman gold coin called a *solidus*). So etymologically, solidarity is not "nice warm feelings of togetherness." It's **collective responsibility that binds you whether you signed up or not**. The whole is liable; the individual is not allowed to opt out.

That legal DNA never really left the word. By the 19th century it had moved from courtrooms into politics and sociology (Pierre Leroux, the utopian socialists, then Émile Durkheim), but the core idea stayed: we are bound together in a way that creates mutual obligations.

### Why it so often feels imposed

Exactly because of that history. When someone today says "show solidarity," they are rarely saying "hey, if you feel like it, join us." They are usually invoking the *in solidum* logic: you are part of the group (workers / the left / the oppressed / the nation / the movement / the decent people), therefore you owe the whole cause. Dissent isn't just disagreement — it's defection, free-riding, betrayal. The etymology makes the moral pressure feel natural.

The origin of the word "solidarity" is deeply embedded in why invocations of it so frequently feel like an imposition rather than a voluntary embrace.

The English term "solidarity" entered the language around 1829, borrowed directly from the French *solidarité*. This was a deliberate neologism introduced in the 1765 *Encyclopédie*, the monumental Enlightenment project edited by Denis Diderot and Jean le Rond d'Alembert. It built upon the older French legal adjective *solidaire*, which itself derived from the Latin phrase *in solidum*—a key concept in Roman law denoting **joint and several liability**. Under this principle, if a group of debtors owed money *in solidum*, each individual was fully responsible for the **entire** debt, not merely a proportional share. No one could evade the obligation by claiming "that's not my portion"—the liability was solid, indivisible, and whole.

The Latin root *solidus* (or neuter *solidum*) means "solid," "whole," "entire," or "undivided." It is the same etymon behind English words like "solid," "consolidate," and even the name of the ancient Roman gold coin, the *solidus*. Etymologically, then, solidarity is far removed from mere sentiments of warmth or camaraderie. It signifies **collective responsibility that binds individuals inescapably**, whether they consented or not. The group forms a single, indivisible unit of obligation; opting out is structurally impossible.

This legal core persisted as the concept migrated from jurisprudence into broader political and sociological discourse during the 19th century—through figures like Pierre Leroux and the utopian socialists, and notably in the work of Émile Durkheim. Yet the fundamental notion endured: human beings are interconnected in ways that impose mutual duties.

This history explains why contemporary appeals to "solidarity" so often carry the weight of compulsion. When someone demands that others "show solidarity," they are seldom extending a casual invitation to join if one feels inclined. Instead, they invoke the *in solidum* logic: you belong to the relevant collective (whether defined as workers, the oppressed, a political movement, a nation, or simply "decent people"), and therefore you bear responsibility for the entire cause. Dissent is recast not as legitimate disagreement but as defection, free-riding, or betrayal. The collective debt is whole; failing to pay one's share jeopardizes the group and makes the individual liable for its shortfalls.

Several recurring contexts illustrate this dynamic:

- **Labor/union context** (classic): "Solidarity forever" sounds beautiful until the moment someone wants to keep working during a strike. Then it becomes "scab," picket line, social ostracism. The debt is collective; crossing it makes you liable for the group's failure.
- **Identity/political movements**: "Solidarity with X" quickly turns into "if you're not actively amplifying us / attending / donating / repeating the slogans, you're part of the problem." The "whole sum" logic reappears: your silence or nuance makes you responsible for the injustice.
- **State/international level**: Governments love the word when they want to justify compulsory policies ("pandemic solidarity," "fiscal solidarity," "migration solidarity"). Suddenly voluntary mutual aid becomes taxes, lockdowns, or quotas you didn't vote for. Critics get called selfish or heartless.

Émile Durkheim's famous distinction remains illuminating here. In smaller, traditional societies, he identified **mechanical solidarity**: cohesion arises from similarity—shared beliefs, values, and ways of life—with deviance harshly repressed because uniformity itself constitutes the social glue. In modern, complex societies, **organic solidarity** prevails: interdependence stems from differentiation and specialization, much like organs in a body (Durkheim, 1893/1984). Yet even in this latter form, the impulse toward enforced conformity resurfaces whenever a subgroup seeks to function as a unified, "solid" entity imposing obligations on its members.


Genuine, voluntary mutual aid certainly exists—neighbors raising barns together, communities rallying after disasters, friends supporting one another without coercion, or grassroots networks operating free of shame tactics. Such practices rarely require grandiose rhetorical framing. In contrast, those who speak loudest about "solidarity" typically seek to expand the collective "whole" for which you are held liable, while narrowing the space where you can say "count me out." The more insistent the sermon on solidarity becomes, the clearer it often is that the aim is to render the obligation non-negotiable—a rhetorical power move cloaked in moral language.

This persistent tension reveals the word's enduring legal inheritance. In essence, solidarity originated as a legal mechanism for holding individuals accountable for the full group debt. Contemporary usage has not erased that DNA; it has merely shifted it from the domain of contract law to that of social and moral pressure. That's precisely why it so often lands as an imposition rather than an embrace. The etymology doesn't lie.
