That’s not luck. That’s **learning by osmosis**.

It’s also what I like to call **learning by focusing** — when you deliberately narrow your attention to one field and let the knowledge come to you instead of frantically hunting for it.

## The Magic of Mini-Serendipities

Once you’re properly “inside” a domain — whether it’s an AI Discord, a research lab, a design community, or even a lively X circle — something beautiful happens. The environment starts feeding you constant tiny moments of serendipity. A random screenshot at 2 a.m., a casual “oh by the way” in a thread, an off-hand comment during a lab coffee break… and suddenly you’ve learned something incredibly useful that you would never have thought to search for.

These aren’t big dramatic breakthroughs. They’re **mini-serendipities** — and they add up fast.

## Polanyi and the Tacit Dimension (the quietly revolutionary idea)

The deeper reason this works so well comes from a philosopher named Michael Polanyi and his book *The Tacit Dimension* (1966). It’s one of those quietly revolutionary books that changes how you see everything once you really get it.

Polanyi’s core insight is simple but profound:

> We can know more than we can tell.

He called this **tacit knowledge** — the kind of knowledge that lives in your intuition, your judgment, your “feel” for things. It’s hard (sometimes impossible) to write down completely. Think of riding a bike, sensing when an AI model is about to go off the rails, or instinctively knowing which workflow just *feels* right.

**Explicit knowledge** is the stuff you can easily write down: tutorials, papers, step-by-step guides, tool lists. That’s what search engines are great at.

**Tacit knowledge** is the invisible layer underneath — the vibe, the shortcuts, the unspoken norms, the pattern recognition that experts have. And you can’t really download it. You have to *absorb* it.

That’s exactly why “just hanging around” feels so disproportionately powerful. You’re not collecting facts — you’re soaking up tacit knowledge through osmosis.

## Where Osmosis Happens Best

This process thrives in what learning theorists call **Communities of Practice** — groups of people who share the same craft or passion and learn together through real participation.

It can look like:
- Online circles (Discord servers, X threads, specialized subreddits)
- Physical labs and research institutes
- Creative scenes and design collectives
- Working groups, recurring meetings, or even casual AI chats

The best ones have something crucial: **psychological safety**. When people feel safe to ask “dumb” questions, share half-baked ideas, or say “I don’t get it yet,” the tacit knowledge flows much more freely.

That’s why some communities feel alive and nourishing, while others feel stiff or performative.

## Even Talking to AI Can Be Osmosis

Here’s a modern twist I love: having casual, ongoing conversations with a good AI (like we’re doing right now) can actually simulate a tiny community of practice. You focus on a topic, throw ideas around, get unexpected connections back — and suddenly you’re absorbing tacit-like insights through dialogue. It’s not the same as human community, but it’s surprisingly effective as a personal osmosis engine.

## Why This Matters So Much Today

We live in a world absolutely flooded with explicit information. Google, YouTube, arXiv, and newsletters throw endless facts at us. But the stuff that actually makes you *good* at something — the intuition, the taste, the practical wisdom — still lives mostly in the tacit layer.

That’s why some people who “study” a field for years still feel like outsiders, while others who simply immerse themselves for a few months start to “get it” on a deeper level.

The highest-leverage skill in 2026 might not be better searching.  
It might be learning how to focus deeply and let yourself soak.
